The Seven Māori Electorate Hīkina Whakatutuki Economic Audit

A Working Programme for Seven Independent Candidates

Prepared by Roderick J. Young, B.Sc., B.Soc.Sc.(Hons), P.G. Dip. (Public Policy), LLB.

1. Purpose

New Zealand has seven Māori electorates.

The purpose of this audit is to recruit and equip seven Independent candidates—one for each Māori electorate—with a common programme for examining:

  • land ownership and control;
  • Crown property;
  • Māori freehold land;
  • minerals and energy resources;
  • housing and infrastructure;
  • employment and business development;
  • iwi and Māori access to investment capital;
  • Treaty settlement outcomes;
  • government expenditure administered through MBIE and other agencies; and
  • the difference between Crown promises and measurable results.

The audit takes its name from MBIE’s Māori identity, Hīkina Whakatutuki, which MBIE broadly translates as:

“Lifting to make successful.”

The political question is simple:

Who has been lifted, by how much, and into whose ownership and control have the resulting assets passed?

2. The National Land Baseline

Aotearoa New Zealand has a total land area of approximately 26.4 million hectares, equivalent to about 65.2 million acres.

Approximately 1.3 million hectares, or about 3.2 million acres, is presently classified as Māori freehold land. This represents approximately 4.9 per cent of New Zealand’s total land area.

It is therefore broadly true that land remaining in the legally recognised Māori freehold category is only a small portion of the national land area. However, it would be too simple to say that all other land was “ceded to the Crown”.

Land passed out of Māori customary ownership through many different mechanisms, including:

  • Crown purchasing;
  • private purchasing after the waiver of Crown pre-emption;
  • decisions and individualisation processes of the Native Land Court;
  • confiscation under nineteenth-century legislation;
  • compulsory acquisition for public works;
  • sales by individual owners;
  • conversion of Māori land into general land;
  • settlement and development schemes;
  • rating pressures;
  • succession fragmentation;
  • leases and incorporations;
  • Treaty settlements; and
  • subsequent private transfers.

The central issue is not merely whether every acre was technically “ceded”.

How did Māori move from customary authority over almost the entire land mass to a position in which Māori freehold land represents approximately 4.9 per cent of that land mass?

That history requires an electorate-by-electorate economic and legal audit.

3. The MBIE Standard

MBIE states that its goal is to create:

“A resilient and high-performing economy that delivers real and ongoing quality of life for all New Zealanders.”

MBIE further states that it is committed to giving effect to Te Tiriti o Waitangi; maintaining enduring and meaningful partnerships with iwi and Māori; addressing longstanding systemic and cross-cutting issues; identifying opportunities for iwi to lead; and creating an enabling environment in which iwi can leverage those opportunities.

Mahi Tahi — Better Together

What decisions have genuinely been made jointly with iwi and Māori?

Māia — Bold and Brave

What bold action has been taken to address land loss, restricted access to capital, housing deprivation and infrastructure deficits?

Pae Kahurangi — Build Our Future

Who will own the housing, energy, minerals, businesses and infrastructure being built?

Pono me te Tika — Own It

Will the Crown publish the complete results of its policies and accept responsibility where outcomes have failed?

6. Electorate Audit One: Te Tai Tokerau

Central question

After nearly two centuries of Crown authority, why does a region of such cultural, natural and strategic importance continue to experience serious deficits in housing, transport, health access and productive infrastructure?

Matters to audit

Māori freehold land by district; landlocked and multiply owned land; Ngāpuhi settlement status; Crown forest land; forestry revenue and ownership; electricity network ownership; road and bridge investment; housing condition and overcrowding; access to business finance; fisheries and aquaculture; water infrastructure; tourism revenue; public-service employment; ports and coastal infrastructure; and MBIE and Kānoa investment.

Candidate’s demand

Publish a complete Crown–Māori economic balance sheet for Northland.

7. Electorate Audit Two: Tāmaki Makaurau

Central question

How can Māori participate fully in the wealth of New Zealand’s largest city when housing costs, rents and land values exclude many Māori families from stable ownership?

Matters to audit

Māori home ownership; rental stress; homelessness; urban Māori authorities; surplus Crown land; Kāinga Ora development; papakāinga barriers; infrastructure charges; Māori business procurement; Māori participation in construction; port and transport assets; access to commercial finance; youth employment; education-to-employment pathways; and ownership of redevelopment projects.

Candidate’s demand

Create a public register showing how much Crown and council land in Auckland has been transferred, leased or made available for Māori housing and enterprise.

8. Electorate Audit Three: Hauraki–Waikato

Central question

Who owns and receives the economic benefit from the land, water, electricity networks, minerals and urban development of the Waikato?

Matters to audit

Māori land remaining within the electorate; Waikato Raupatu settlement assets; Waikato River governance; electricity distribution and generation; WEL Networks and related community ownership; coal and mineral resources; rural land conversion; Hamilton housing growth; water infrastructure; rates and local-government debt; Treaty settlement redress; Crown property; Māori business lending; regional transport; and MBIE economic-development expenditure.

Candidate’s demand

Produce a Waikato resource-ownership map showing the beneficial owners of electricity, land, water-use rights, minerals, major housing developments and public infrastructure.

9. Electorate Audit Four: Waiariki

Central question

Why have regions rich in forests, geothermal energy, tourism, water and productive land not produced secure prosperity for every community living within them?

Matters to audit

Geothermal ownership and royalties; forestry ownership and processing; tourism income; Māori land utilisation; housing; energy hardship; regional roads; Rotorua development; Eastern Bay of Plenty deprivation; aquaculture; Treaty settlement assets; Māori trusts and incorporations; access to development finance; and government investment administered through Kānoa.

Candidate’s demand

Require a regional natural-resource dividend statement showing where geothermal, forestry, tourism and water revenues ultimately flow.

10. Electorate Audit Five: Te Tai Hauāuru

Central question

How can landowners exercise real economic authority when land fragmentation, finance restrictions, infrastructure deficits and regulatory costs obstruct development?

Matters to audit

Māori land utilisation; farming and horticulture; forestry; Whanganui River arrangements; Taranaki energy resources; petroleum and gas; electricity infrastructure; housing; coastal resilience; rail and road connections; regional hospitals; Māori business capital; public works acquisitions; and Treaty settlement implementation.

Candidate’s demand

Establish an independent review of barriers preventing collectively owned Māori land from securing development finance on fair terms.

11. Electorate Audit Six: Ikaroa–Rāwhiti

Central question

Why do communities surrounded by forestry, farming, fisheries and renewable-energy potential remain vulnerable to poverty, isolation and infrastructure failure?

Matters to audit

Forestry ownership; raw-log exports; local wood processing; road resilience; cyclone recovery; housing; insurance availability; coastal land; fisheries; port access; electricity reliability; public works; health and maternity services; youth employment; Treaty settlements; and Crown recovery funding.

Candidate’s demand

Require all major Crown-funded regional projects to disclose how much expenditure, employment and ownership remains permanently within the region.

12. Electorate Audit Seven: Te Tai Tonga

Central question

Who should benefit from the immense land, mineral, energy, fisheries and conservation estate of the South Island and the Chatham Islands?

Matters to audit

Ngāi Tahu settlement assets; Crown pastoral land; conservation land; lignite and coal resources; hydroelectric generation; transmission infrastructure; fisheries quota; aquaculture; ports; tourism concessions; housing; the Chatham Islands; transport resilience; mineral exploration; regional processing industries; and Crown ownership.

Candidate’s demand

Publish a South Island resource ledger identifying the ownership, regulatory control, royalties, rents and ultimate beneficiaries of major natural resources.

13. The Ten Questions for MBIE

  1. How much has MBIE invested in Māori economic development within each Māori electorate during the last ten financial years?
  2. Which iwi, hapū, Māori organisations and Māori-owned businesses received that investment?
  3. What proportion was provided as grants, loans, underwriting, equity or advisory support?
  4. What permanent assets were created?
  5. Who owns those assets now?
  6. How many permanent jobs resulted?
  7. What proportion of procurement expenditure went to Māori-owned businesses?
  8. How does MBIE measure whether an iwi has been enabled to lead?
  9. What barriers to using Māori land as productive capital has MBIE identified?
  10. What failures has MBIE recorded, and what corrective action followed?

14. The Independent Programme

A national Māori economic ledger

A publicly accessible account of Māori land, Crown land, settlement assets, natural resources, government investment and measurable economic outcomes.

Electorate-level reporting

Government expenditure affecting Māori should be reported by Māori electorate as well as by agency and administrative region.

Transparent Crown land records

Surplus, leased, acquired and disposed Crown land should be mapped, including the history of former Māori ownership where records allow.

Fair access to capital

A review should examine whether Māori landowners, trusts, incorporations and businesses face structural barriers in banking and development finance.

Local ownership of resources

Economic policy should favour durable local participation and ownership rather than merely temporary employment during construction.

Infrastructure as an economic right

Roads, telecommunications, water, electricity and housing are not peripheral social programmes. They are the foundation upon which productive participation depends.

Auditable Treaty performance

“Giving effect to Te Tiriti” must be accompanied by published objectives, budgets, timeframes and outcomes.

15. Political Position

The seven candidates would not stand as a racial bloc or as an anti-Māori bloc.

They would stand as Independents asking whether the Crown’s promises can survive an audit.

Candidates who are Pākehā and unable to speak Māori may lawfully stand. Nevertheless, legal eligibility is only the beginning.

  • humility;
  • knowledge;
  • respect for tikanga and local communities;
  • willingness to listen;
  • accurate use of evidence;
  • openness about their limitations;
  • independence from party machines; and
  • commitment to representing everyone in the electorate.
Judge us by whether we listen, whether we tell the truth, whether we investigate the Crown, and whether we obtain measurable results.

16. Parliamentary Objective

Winning seven electorate seats would not automatically give the Independent group the right to appoint a Prime Minister.

The constitutional question would be whether a person could command the confidence of a majority in the House of Representatives.

The seven Independent MPs could, however, become decisive where neither major political grouping held a majority.

  1. completion of the seven-electorate economic audit;
  2. annual public reporting by MBIE;
  3. a Crown and natural-resource ownership ledger;
  4. reform of Māori development finance;
  5. electorate-level housing and infrastructure plans;
  6. transparent ministerial accountability; and
  7. a right for Independent electorate MPs to publish formal assessments of Crown performance.

17. Shadow Executive

Before asking who should become Prime Minister, each proposed candidate should answer four questions:

  1. What portfolio are you competent to hold?
  2. What relevant experience do you possess?
  3. What would you change during your first 100 days?
  4. What evidence would prove that you had succeeded?

18. Final Challenge

Mahi Tahi — Better Together. Then publish the accounts.

Māia — Bold and Brave. Then confront the history.

Pae Kahurangi — Build Our Future. Then disclose who will own what is built.

Pono me te Tika — Own It. Then accept responsibility for the result.

Hīkina Whakatutuki cannot remain a departmental identity printed above business-as-usual policy. It must become an enforceable standard of public performance.

Seven electorates.
Seven Independent candidates.
One national audit.

After 186 years, where is the enabling environment?
